Your Data, Their Profit

by: Kenji Morales

The Hidden Cost of Convenience

In today's digital economy, personal data has become the most valuable commodity—often traded behind the scenes without the user's full awareness. Every click, scroll, and search feeds into vast databases that power targeted advertising, predictive algorithms, and behavioral profiling. While users enjoy free access to platforms, the real cost is their privacy. Companies monetize this data to optimize engagement, influence purchasing decisions, and even shape political opinions.

The problem isn't just the collection of data—it's the lack of transparency and consent. Many platforms bury their data practices in lengthy terms and conditions that few users read. According to Cyber Rights Organization, this opaque system undermines digital autonomy and violates basic cyber rights such as the right to privacy and informed consent (Cyber Rights Organization, n.d.). The imbalance of power between tech corporations and users has created a digital landscape where exploitation is normalized.

As artificial intelligence and machine learning evolve, the stakes grow higher. Algorithms trained on personal data can predict behavior, manipulate emotions, and reinforce biases. This raises ethical concerns about surveillance capitalism—a system where profit is prioritized over human dignity. Harvard Business Review emphasizes the need for companies to build cultures that take cybersecurity seriously, using behavioral nudges and public commitments to protect user data (Muhly et al., 2025).

Governments and watchdogs are beginning to respond, but regulation often lags behind innovation. The European Union;s Digital Rights Declaration frames cyber rights as human rights, including the right to be forgotten and protection of minors online (Cyber Rights Organization, n.d.). In the Philippines, legal experts stress the importance of privacy laws and digital accountability, especially in cases involving defamation, workplace surveillance, and unauthorized data sharing (Singh, 2025).

Ultimately, reclaiming digital agency requires more than policy—it demands public awareness and advocacy. Users must be empowered to question platforms, demand transparency, and choose tools that respect their rights. Privacy isn't just a technical issue—it's a social one. And in a world where data equals power, protecting it is an act of resistance.
